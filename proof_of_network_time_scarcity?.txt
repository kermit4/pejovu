2. *Latency ticket*: Each node computes `ticket = hash(R || my_ed25519h || median_latency_to_k_peers)`. Low ticket wins.

*A) Can one Sybil with many IPs fake delays to fool me?*

You can even be consistent: if I ping you 100 times from my IP, you always reply in 87ms ? 2ms. Looks legit.

*The attack*: Buy 1000 VPSs in 50 countries. When I ask "WhereAreThey" for key `K`, you give me the IP in Frankfurt. When I ping it, your Frankfurt box replies with 20ms delay to me in NYC. Perfect.

So sampling 1 signer proves nothing. A Sybil with global infrastructure passes.

*B) Does sampling _my_ random peers vs DAG peers save me?*

*Yes. This is the key.* You don't randomly sample from the DAG. You randomly sample from _your_ view of the network, then check if the DAG's claims about those peers are consistent.

Here's why that breaks the Sybil:

*The Triangle Inequality Attack*

Latency obeys physics: `RTT(A,C) <= RTT(A,B) + RTT(B,C)`. Speed of light + routing.

*Setup*: Attacker has 1000 IPs, but only in 3 datacenters: Virginia, Frankfurt, Singapore. They generate 10k keys and claim diverse geo distribution.

*Your defense*:
1. You already know 200 real peers from normal gossip. You've pinged them over time. You know `RTT(you -> peer_i)` for 200 real IPs.
2. New DAG shows up with 500 attestations. You ignore their IP list. You pick 20 peers _you already know_ that also appear in the DAG.
3. For each, you check: "DAG claims peer `i` has RTT 15ms to peer `j`. I know my RTT to `i` is 40ms and my RTT to `j` is 45ms. Is 15ms possible?"

*The break*: The attacker's 10k keys all route through 3 datacenters. So for any two fake peers `X` and `Y`, the real path is `you -> Virginia -> X` and `you -> Virginia -> Y`.

That means `RTT(you,X)` ? `RTT(you,Y)` ? `RTT(you, Virginia)`. The DAG will claim `RTT(X,Y) = 5ms` because the attacker wants them to look "close".

But you measure `RTT(you,X)=60ms`, `RTT(you,Y)=62ms`. Triangle inequality says `RTT(X,Y) >= |60-62| = 2ms` and `<= 60+62 = 122ms`. 5ms is plausible.

Now add peer `Z` where DAG claims `RTT(X,Z)=5ms` and `RTT(Y,Z)=200ms`. You measure `RTT(you,Z)=61ms`.

Check: `|60-61|=1ms <= 5ms <= 121ms` OK. `|62-61|=1ms <= 200ms <= 123ms` OK. Still passes.

*But now add 20 peers.* The attacker has to assign fake RTTs to `C(20,2) = 190` pairs. All 20 IPs are actually in Virginia. So all real RTTs from you are ~60ms ? 3ms.

To look diverse, attacker claims some pairs are 10ms apart, some 150ms apart. But you can test: if `RTT(X,Y)_claimed=10ms` and `RTT(X,Z)_claimed=150ms`, then triangle inequality requires `RTT(Y,Z)` between 140ms and 160ms. If the DAG claims 5ms, it's busted.

With 20 points, the probability the attacker can assign 190 distances that _all_ satisfy triangle inequality + match your 20 measurements is basically 0, unless they actually have 20 machines spread globally.

*This is embedding + outlier detection.* You're using your 200 known peers as landmarks. The DAG is proposing a coordinate system for new peers. If the new peers are fake, their coordinates won't embed into real internet latency space.

*The crypto+stats mixup, concretely*

*Cryptography part*: Signatures prove "key K attests RTT to key J was 45ms at time T". Unforgeable.

*Statistics part*: Given 20 unforgeable attestations from peers you independently trust, is there a physical arrangement of those peers on Earth that explains all 190 pairwise distances?

That's a `O(n^2)` constraint system. A Sybil with `m` real locations can satisfy at most `O(m^2)` constraints. To fake `n=1000` diverse peers, you need `m?1000` real boxes globally. Cost = PoW.

*Your sampling strategy:*
1. Maintain peer table: {ed25519h -> IP, RTT_samples[50], last_seen}
2. When new DAG arrives, intersect: DAG_keys ? your_peer_table = candidates
3. If |candidates| < 10: reject, not enough overlap to audit
4. Pick 20 random from candidates
5. For each pair i,j: get DAG_claim_RTT(i,j), compute your_RTT(i) and your_RTT(j)
6. Check: |your_RTT(i) - your_RTT(j)| <= DAG_claim_RTT(i,j) <= your_RTT(i) + your_RTT(j)
7. If >15% violations: reject DAG as Sybil
8. Optional: Run MDS embedding on the 20x20 matrix. If stress > 0.3, reject.
A real global network embeds into 2D+latency with stress <0.2. A Sybil with 3 datacenters has stress >0.8. You can detect it in 5ms of CPU.

*So answering A vs B directly:*

*A) Can they fake delays to match data regardless of where you sample from?*
No. They can fake delays to _you_. They can't fake delays _between their Sybil nodes_ that remain consistent with your measurements to each node individually. Geometry breaks them.

*B) Does sampling from your peer data vs DAG data matter?*
Yes, it's everything. If you only sample from the DAG, the Sybil gives you 20 IPs all in Virginia and you learn nothing. If you sample from _your known peers_ that happen to be in the DAG, the Sybil has to predict which 20 you'll pick and pre-position boxes near all your peers.
